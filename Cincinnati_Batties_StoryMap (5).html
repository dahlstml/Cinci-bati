Quick quiz — no googling!

🦇 True or False: Bats are blind.

FALSE! All bat species can see. But Indiana bats also use echolocation to navigate and hunt in the dark — emitting high-pitched sounds and listening to the echoes that bounce back. Scientists call it nature's sonar. We call it an incredible bonus superpower. 🦇✨

Source: Bat Conservation International · batcon.org

The challenges

Okay, here is the sad part 😢

Indiana bats are dealing with a lot right now. But knowing what is happening is the first step to fixing it!

🍄

White Nose Syndrome

Since 2006, a fungal disease called White Nose Syndrome (caused by Pseudogymnoascus destructans) has been sweeping through bat hibernation sites. It grows on hibernating bats, messes with their sleep, and forces them to burn through their fat reserves before spring arrives. Indiana bats hibernate packed together in huge groups — which makes them devastatingly vulnerable. A single infected cave can experience catastrophic losses in one season.

An estimated 6 to 7 million bats have died since WNS first emerged. (Frick et al., 2016)

Source: whitenosesyndrome.org · Frick et al., Science 2016

🌳

Disappearing Habitat

The mature riparian forests Indiana bats depend on have been shrinking for decades as Cincinnati has grown. Habitat fragmentation removes the large old trees bat colonies roost under and pushes populations into smaller and smaller patches of forest.

Source: USGS National Land Cover Database

🏠

Accidental Harm

Here is the surprising part — most harm is accidental! Homeowners seal up house gaps not knowing a bat colony is roosting inside. Hikers explore caves in winter and carry fungal spores on their gear. Tree crews remove dead trees during bat pup season. Nobody is trying to hurt these animals. They just do not know the bats are there — and that is exactly what this project is here to change!

Source: Prokop & Tunnicliffe, 2008

🦇 Indiana Bat
Myotis sodalis
⚠️ Federally Endangered since 1967

The Indiana bat is a small, insect-eating mammal found throughout the eastern United States — including right here in the greater Cincinnati region. Despite being listed as federally endangered since 1967, most Cincinnatians have no idea this species exists in their backyard. That is actually part of the problem.

They weigh between 4.5 and 9.5 grams — about as much as three pennies — with a wingspan of 9 to 11 inches. Their fur is dark brown to black. They eat moths, beetles, flies, and other night-flying insects — basically all the bugs you would rather not deal with.

✨ Superpower: Uses echolocation so precise it can detect an object as thin as a human hair from 10 meters away in total darkness — that is the length of a school bus, in complete darkness. (Source: Neighbourhood Bat Watch / National Geographic)

You and the bats

The Human Connection 🤝

If bats make you a little nervous, you are absolutely not alone! Bats have a bit of a public relations problem. Centuries of association with darkness and spooky mythology have not exactly done them any favors. Research has found that fear and disgust responses toward bats are actually among the biggest barriers to bat conservation engagement. (Prokop and Tunnicliffe, 2008)

"Fear is not permanent — and you do not have to love bats to help them. You just have to know they are there."

Cincinnati sits within one of the most important regions for Indiana bat summer habitat in all of Ohio. (USFWS, 2007) The river valleys you bike along, kayak through, and walk your dog beside are bat highways. The old trees in your neighborhood park are potential roost sites. These animals are woven into the fabric of this city, quietly doing their work in the dark while we go about our lives.

🌙 The biggest threats to Indiana bats in this region are ones that local people can genuinely do something about. You do not need a science degree or a government grant. You just need to know they are there — and now you do.
You can help!

What YOU can do 🙌

You do not have to be a scientist to make a difference. Here is where to start!

1
Stay out of caves between October and April 🕳️

This is honestly the single most impactful thing a recreational hiker or caver can do. Visiting hibernation sites during this window wakes bats up at the worst possible time and spreads White Nose Syndrome fungal spores between locations on clothing and gear. If you visit caves year-round, follow the free decontamination protocols at whitenosesyndrome.org.

2
Hold off on tree removal between May and August 🌳

Indiana bat maternity season runs roughly through those months, and large dead or dying trees that look like yard hazards may be active roost sites. Ohio DNR has great guidance and can connect you with a licensed wildlife professional if you need help.

3
Put up a bat box! 🏠

Bat boxes provide alternative roosting habitat and are surprisingly easy to install. Place them 10 to 15 feet high on a south or southeast-facing structure near water. Bat Conservation International has free plans and placement guides ready for you.

4
Reduce pesticides in your yard 🌿

Indiana bats are insect-eating machines, but pesticide-heavy landscapes cut down on the prey base they depend on — especially after a tough winter dealing with White Nose Syndrome. Going low-pesticide is a win for bats and for your whole local ecosystem.

5
Report your bat sightings on iNaturalist! 📍

Citizen science data genuinely helps researchers track where Indiana bat populations still exist. If you spot a bat near a river corridor, log it at inaturalist.org. Every single observation matters — and you can join the Cinci-BATi iNaturalist project to see what your neighbors are spotting too!

The Indiana bat does not need you to love it. It just needs you to know it is there.

Cincinnati has a lot going for it: its river views, its neighborhoods, its very passionate opinions about chili. But it also has something most people do not know about — a front-row seat to one of the most urgent bat conservation stories in North America. The next time you are walking along the Little Miami at dusk and something small and fast darts overhead? That is your neighbor. Say hi.
